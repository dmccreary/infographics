    Five Steps in Brewing: Detailed Image Prompts

    Each prompt is designed for a text-to-image model. The five images form a visual narrative that follows a single glucose molecule from crushed grain through to ethanol in the finished beer. Every image uses the same virtual "camera" — a cutaway of a stainless-steel conical fermenter in a dimly lit cellar — so students see continuity across the sequence. Emphasize vivid cellular and equipment detail, cinematic lighting, and no embedded text, labels, numbers, arrows, callout lines, or annotation marks anywhere in the image. The diagram overlay architecture will annotate elements later.

    Consistent visual elements across all five images:

    • Stainless-steel conical fermenter, partially cut away to reveal contents
    • Dim amber cellar lighting from the upper left
    • A magnified inset circle (lower-right quadrant) showing the molecular-scale view inside a single yeast cell's cytoplasm
    • Aspect ratio: landscape 2:1, 1200 x 600 px
    • Style: biological textbook illustration with clean line art, light color fills, white background inside the molecular inset, AP Biology high school level
    • Palette continuity: glucose = warm gold, pyruvate = orange-red, acetaldehyde = salmon pink, ethanol = pale blue-green, ATP = bright yellow glow, NADH = violet glow, NAD+ = gray, CO2 = translucent white bubbles, yeast cell walls = tan/khaki, cytoplasm = pale cream

    Step 1 — Glucose Feedstock Preparation (Mashing and Wort Production)

    What is happening: Malted barley is crushed and mixed with hot water in a mash tun. Amylase enzymes from the malt break long starch chains into fermentable sugars — primarily maltose (two glucose units) and free glucose. The resulting sugar-rich liquid, called wort, is drained into a brew kettle, boiled with hops, cooled, and transferred into the fermenter. This step provides the raw fuel that yeast will consume.

    Key molecules to show: Starch polymer chains (long spirals of linked hexagons), amylase enzyme (wedge shape cleaving the chain), maltose (two linked hexagonal rings), free glucose (single hexagonal ring glowing warm gold), water molecules surrounding everything.

Inside the mash tun, show crushed malted barley grains splitting open, releasing pale starch granules into steaming hot water. Magnified inset circle in the lower-right corner zooms into the endosperm of a single barley grain at molecular scale: long coiled starch polymer chains (light tan spirals of linked hexagonal glucose units) being cleaved by wedge-shaped amylase enzymes (blue-green) into short maltose disaccharides (two linked gold hexagons) and free glucose monomers (single gold hexagons glowing warmly). Water molecules (small transparent spheres) surround the reaction. Outside the inset, show the golden wort flowing through a plate heat exchanger, steam rising, and a few oval yeast cells (tan walls, pale cream interior) floating near the fermenter inlet, sensing the sugar gradient. Stainless piping, pressure gauges, and a thermometer are visible on the equipment. Warm amber cellar lighting from the upper left. Image size: 1200 x 600 pixels, landscape 2:1 aspect ratio.     Step 2 — Glycolysis Inside the Fermenter

    What is happening: Yeast cells absorb glucose from the wort and immediately begin glycolysis in their cytoplasm. This is a ten-enzyme cascade that splits each 6-carbon glucose molecule into two 3-carbon pyruvate molecules. The process nets 2 ATP molecules (the cell's energy currency) and 2 NADH molecules (electron carriers that will be important in Step 5). Glycolysis does not require oxygen — it proceeds whether or not the fermenter is sealed.

    Key molecules to show: Glucose (6-carbon gold hexagonal ring) entering the yeast cell, a chain of enzyme complexes processing it through intermediate forms, two pyruvate molecules (3-carbon, orange-red triangular shapes) emerging at the end, 2 ATP (bright yellow glowing spheres), 2 NADH (violet glowing spheres), faint mitochondria pushed to the cell periphery.

    Prompt:

    Please generate a new image. Interior view of a stainless conical fermenter filled with amber wort, hundreds of tiny CO2 bubbles beginning to form along the vessel walls. The fermenter is cut away to reveal the liquid inside. Dozens of oval Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast cells are suspended in the wort, each with visible tan cell walls and pale cream cytoplasm. Magnified inset circle in the lower-right corner zooms into the cytoplasm of a single yeast cell at molecular scale: a warm-gold hexagonal glucose molecule enters from the left through a transporter channel in the cell membrane, then passes through a conveyor-like chain of ten small enzyme complexes (shown as distinct colored shapes arranged left to right), being progressively reshaped at each step. At the midpoint, the 6-carbon molecule splits into two 3-carbon fragments. At the far right of the chain, two orange-red triangular pyruvate molecules emerge. Above the enzyme chain, two bright yellow glowing ATP spheres and two violet glowing NADH spheres float upward, representing the energy harvested. In the background of the cell, faint oval mitochondria with internal cristae folds are pushed to the cell periphery, inactive. Outside the inset, show instrumentation probes (temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen) inserted through the fermenter wall, digital readout panels on the outside, and stainless sample valves.     Step 3 — Pyruvate Accumulation Under Anaerobic Conditions

    What is happening: The brewer seals the fermenter, and the yeast quickly consumes any remaining dissolved oxygen. With oxygen gone, the mitochondrial electron transport chain shuts down — pyruvate cannot enter the mitochondria for aerobic respiration. Pyruvate molecules accumulate in the cytoplasm. Meanwhile, NADH also accumulates because it has no way to donate its electrons to the ETC. This creates a metabolic bottleneck: if NADH is not recycled back to NAD+, glycolysis will stall for lack of NAD+. Fermentation is the cell's solution to this problem (Steps 4 and 5).

    Key molecules to show: Multiple pyruvate molecules (orange-red triangles) crowding the cytoplasm, NADH molecules (violet glow) accumulating, mitochondria with closed/blocked pores (red X marks on the outer membrane channels), NAD+ (gray, depleted) becoming scarce.

    Prompt:

    Please generate a new image. The same conical fermenter, now fully sealed with a tri-clamp lid and a blow-off hose running from the top into a bucket of sanitizer water. A CO2 headspace blanket is visible above the wort surface as a faint haze. The fermenter is cut away to show yeast cells suspended in darkening amber wort. Magnified inset circle in the lower-right corner zooms into the cytoplasm of a single yeast cell: the cytoplasm is now crowded with many orange-red triangular pyruvate molecules that have nowhere to go. Several violet glowing NADH spheres are accumulating in clusters. Along the right side of the cell, two mitochondria are shown with their outer membrane channels visibly blocked — the channel pores are closed with small red barrier marks, and the interior cristae are dim and inactive. A few gray NAD+ spheres (depleted, no glow) are scattered sparsely, showing they are running out. A faint dotted arrow-like visual trail from pyruvate toward the blocked mitochondria emphasizes the dead end, rendered purely as a fading color gradient (no actual arrow or text). Outside the inset, show the sealed fermenter lid, pressure relief valve, the blow-off hose with bubbles beginning to form in the sanitizer bucket, and dim cellar lighting with cool blue-gray tones to emphasize the anaerobic shift. Temperature controller on the outside shows the vessel is being chilled. No text, labels, numbers, arrows, or annotation marks of any kind anywhere in the image.

    Step 4 — Decarboxylation: Acetaldehyde and CO2 Release

    What is happening: The enzyme pyruvate decarboxylase (PDC) removes one carbon from each 3-carbon pyruvate, releasing it as carbon dioxide (CO2). What remains is acetaldehyde, a 2-carbon molecule. This is the step that produces all the CO2 in beer — it carbonates the beer naturally, creates the foam head, and is collected through blow-off tubes. Each original glucose yields two pyruvates, so two CO2 molecules are released per glucose. Acetaldehyde has a green-apple off-flavor that must be cleaned up in Step 5.

    Key molecules to show: Pyruvate decarboxylase enzyme complex (large, multi-lobed protein), pyruvate (orange-red triangle) entering the enzyme active site, CO2 (small translucent white sphere) being released and floating upward, acetaldehyde (2-carbon salmon-pink shape) exiting the enzyme, the cofactor TPP (thiamine pyrophosphate, small green diamond) nestled in the enzyme active site.

    Step 5 — Ethanol Formation and NAD+ Regeneration (Conditioning)

    What is happening: The enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) transfers electrons from NADH to acetaldehyde, reducing it to ethanol — the alcohol in beer. This reaction simultaneously regenerates NAD+ from NADH, which is the true biological purpose of fermentation: recycling NAD+ so glycolysis can continue producing ATP. Without this step, NADH would accumulate, NAD+ would run out, and glycolysis would stop. The yeast eventually flocculates (clumps and settles) to the bottom cone. The bright beer is transferred to a conditioning tank where residual acetaldehyde is cleaned up, CO2 is captured, and flavors mature.

    Key molecules to show: Alcohol dehydrogenase enzyme (large, multi-lobed protein), acetaldehyde (salmon-pink V-shape) entering the active site, NADH (violet glow) donating electrons (shown as a light trail transferring from NADH to acetaldehyde), ethanol (pale blue-green small molecule) exiting, NAD+ (now restored to gray, ready for reuse) departing the enzyme, a circular recycling motif showing NAD+ flowing back toward the glycolysis enzymes from Step 2.
