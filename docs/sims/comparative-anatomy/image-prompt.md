# Comparative Anatomy — Forelimb Homology Image Prompt

## Purpose

Generate a single wide illustration showing the forelimbs of five vertebrate species side by side. The image will be used as a background for an interactive MicroSim where students hover over bones to learn about homologous structures. **No text, labels, numbers, arrows, callout lines, leader lines, or annotation marks anywhere in the image.**

## Global Specifications

- **Aspect ratio:** 5:2 landscape (1500 × 600 px)
- **Style:** Clean biological textbook illustration, precise anatomical line art with flat color fills, white background, AP Biology high school level
- **Layout:** Five equal-width vertical panels arranged horizontally, separated by thin light-gray divider lines. Each panel contains one species forelimb.
- **Orientation:** All forelimbs oriented vertically with the shoulder/proximal end at the top and digits/distal end at the bottom
- **Background:** Pure white within each panel
- **No text of any kind in the image. No labels, no species names, no bone names, no numbers, no arrows, no annotation marks. Repeat: absolutely no text.**

## Panel 1 — Human Arm (leftmost)

Draw a human right arm from shoulder to fingertips, anterior (palm-forward) view, showing internal skeletal structure through a semi-transparent flesh silhouette.

- **Flesh silhouette:** Very light peach/skin tone (#FDEBD0), 15% opacity outline of the full arm shape — muscular upper arm tapering to a narrower forearm, widening slightly at the wrist, then five distinct spread fingers
- **Humerus (red):** Single long bone running from the top of the panel down to the elbow region, roughly 35% of the total limb length. Slightly wider at the proximal (shoulder) end, narrowing at the shaft, widening again at the distal (elbow) end with visible condyles
- **Radius (medium blue):** On the lateral (thumb) side of the forearm. Narrower at the elbow, wider at the wrist. Runs from elbow to wrist, about 30% of limb length
- **Ulna (dark blue):** On the medial (pinky) side. Prominent olecranon process (elbow point) at the top. Wider at the elbow, tapering toward the wrist. Slightly longer than the radius
- **Carpals (green):** Cluster of 8 small irregular rounded bones at the wrist, arranged in two rows of four. Each carpal bone individually outlined
- **Phalanges (yellow):** Five metacarpals fanning out from the carpals, each leading to 3 phalanges (2 for the thumb). The fingers are spread apart, clearly showing all 14 phalanges as individual bone segments connected at visible joints. The thumb is shorter and offset

**Key visual:** Long, straight proportions. Fingers long and separated. This is the "generalized" mammalian forelimb plan.

## Panel 2 — Whale Flipper

Draw a right whale flipper in lateral view, showing internal skeletal structure through a semi-transparent flipper silhouette.

- **Flipper silhouette:** Light blue-gray (#D6EAF8), smooth paddle shape — narrow at the shoulder, broadening dramatically into a wide, rounded paddle with no visible finger separation on the exterior
- **Humerus (red):** Extremely short and thick — almost a squat rectangle. Only about 10-12% of the total flipper length. Very wide relative to its length. Positioned at the very top of the flipper
- **Radius (medium blue):** Short and flattened into a wide disc-like shape. Positioned side by side with the ulna below the humerus
- **Ulna (dark blue):** Similar short, wide, flattened shape as the radius. Together they look like two wide plates rather than long rods
- **Carpals (green):** Small rounded bones clustered in a compact group below radius/ulna. Fewer and more reduced than in the human
- **Phalanges (yellow):** This is where the whale flipper is dramatically different — the metacarpals and phalanges are greatly elongated and numerous (hyperphalangy). Show 5 rays of many small yellow bone segments fanning out and filling the entire broad paddle area. The individual phalanges are small rectangular segments stacked in long chains. They occupy about 60-65% of the total flipper length. The rays spread widely to fill the paddle shape

**Key visual:** Tiny humerus, tiny radius/ulna, but enormously elongated phalanges filling a broad paddle. The bones are embedded inside a smooth, teardrop paddle shape.

## Panel 3 — Bat Wing

Draw a right bat wing from shoulder to wingtip, dorsal view with the wing partially extended, showing internal skeletal structure through a semi-transparent wing membrane.

- **Wing membrane (patagium):** Very light purple (#F5EEF8), thin translucent membrane stretching between the elongated fingers. The membrane extends from the body (left edge) out to the fingertips and down to where the leg would be
- **Humerus (red):** Short and robust, about 15% of total wing length. Thick bone angled slightly outward from the body
- **Radius (medium blue):** Long and thin — much longer than the humerus. About 25% of wing span. This is the dominant forearm bone in bats
- **Ulna (dark blue):** Very reduced and thin, running parallel to but much shorter than the radius. Barely visible — just a thin sliver alongside the radius
- **Carpals (green):** Small cluster of bones at the wrist joint where the wing "bends"
- **Phalanges (yellow):** This is the most dramatic feature — four enormously elongated finger rays (digits 2-5) spread out like umbrella spokes supporting the wing membrane. Each finger has 2-3 very long, thin phalanges. The longest finger (digit 3) extends to the wingtip and is about 50% of the total wing length. Show the fingers spread apart with the translucent membrane visible between them. One short thumb with a claw visible at the wrist bend (not part of the wing membrane)

**Key visual:** Short humerus, long radius, vestigial ulna, and spectacularly elongated finger bones spreading apart like tent poles supporting the membrane. The wing shape is clearly visible.

## Panel 4 — Dog Forelimb

Draw a right dog (medium-sized breed like a Labrador) forelimb from shoulder to paw, lateral view, showing internal skeletal structure through a semi-transparent leg silhouette.

- **Leg silhouette:** Light tan/buff (#FAE5D3), muscular at the shoulder tapering to a slender lower leg, ending in a compact paw with visible toe pads
- **Humerus (red):** Long and robust with prominent muscle attachment ridges (deltoid tuberosity visible as a bump). About 30% of limb length. Angled slightly backward from vertical
- **Radius (medium blue):** Straight and strong, weight-bearing. Runs from elbow to wrist, about 28% of limb length. The radius is the primary weight-bearing forearm bone
- **Ulna (dark blue):** Runs parallel to the radius with a prominent olecranon (elbow point) projecting upward behind the humerus. Slightly thinner than the radius but similar length
- **Carpals (green):** Compact cluster of small bones. The dog "wrist" is what most people think is the dog's "knee" on the front leg — positioned high above the paw
- **Phalanges (yellow):** Short and compact. Four main toes (digits 2-5) with a small dewclaw (digit 1) higher up. Each toe has 3 short phalanges. The dog walks on its toes (digitigrade), so the metacarpals are elongated and nearly vertical, with the phalanges angled to meet the ground. Show small claw shapes at the tips

**Key visual:** Proportionally similar to human but with the limb adapted for running — the "wrist" is high up, the animal walks on its toes, and the bones are robust for weight bearing.

## Panel 5 — Bird Wing (rightmost)

Draw a right bird wing (eagle or hawk) partially extended, dorsal view, showing internal skeletal structure through a semi-transparent wing silhouette with feather outlines.

- **Wing silhouette:** Very light green (#EAFAF1), elongated airfoil shape with visible feather outlines along the trailing edge — long primary flight feathers at the tip, shorter secondary feathers along the inner wing
- **Humerus (red):** Short, thick, and hollow-looking (show it slightly lighter/translucent to suggest pneumatic bone). About 20% of wing length. Held close to the body
- **Radius (medium blue):** Long and thin, running from elbow to wrist. About 30% of wing length
- **Ulna (dark blue):** Slightly thicker than the radius and curved. The secondary flight feathers attach along the ulna. Show small bumps (quill knobs) along the ulna where feather shafts anchor
- **Carpals (green):** Greatly reduced — only two small bones visible at the wrist. These are fused in birds (carpometacarpus), so show them as a compact unit
- **Phalanges (yellow):** Dramatically reduced and fused. Only three digits remain, and they are mostly fused into a single pointed structure (the alula digit is separate and small). Show the fused metacarpals and reduced phalanges as a single tapering yellow structure at the wingtip. The primary flight feathers attach here. This is strikingly different from the bat — instead of long separate fingers, the bird has a rigid fused hand

**Key visual:** Hollow humerus, prominent ulna with feather attachments, and a fused/reduced hand — the opposite strategy from the bat. The wing is rigid and feathered rather than flexible and membranous.

## Composition Notes

- All five limbs should be drawn at approximately the same total height within their panels so they can be visually compared
- The shoulder/proximal end should align horizontally across all five panels
- Each limb should be centered horizontally within its panel
- Leave enough white space around each limb for overlay markers (at least 15% padding on each side)
- The color fills must be flat and solid — no gradients, no shading, no drop shadows on the bones
- The semi-transparent flesh/membrane silhouettes should be just visible enough to show the overall limb shape without obscuring the bone colors
- Bone outlines should be clean, 1-2px dark gray lines with no sketchy or hand-drawn effect

**No text, labels, numbers, arrows, callout lines, leader lines, or annotation marks anywhere in the image. This is critical — the interactive overlay adds all labels programmatically.**
