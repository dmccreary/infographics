# Five Steps in Brewing: Detailed Image Prompts

Each prompt is designed for a text-to-image model. The five images form a
**visual narrative** that follows a single glucose molecule from crushed grain
through to ethanol in the finished beer. Every image uses the same virtual
"camera" — a cutaway of a stainless-steel conical fermenter in a dimly lit
cellar — so students see continuity across the sequence. Emphasize vivid
cellular and equipment detail, cinematic lighting, and **no embedded text,
labels, numbers, arrows, callout lines, or annotation marks anywhere in the
image**. The diagram overlay architecture will annotate elements later.

**Consistent visual elements across all five images:**

- Stainless-steel conical fermenter, partially cut away to reveal contents
- Dim amber cellar lighting from the upper left
- A magnified inset circle (lower-right quadrant) showing the molecular-scale
  view inside a single yeast cell's cytoplasm
- Aspect ratio: landscape 2:1, 1200 x 600 px
- Style: biological textbook illustration with clean line art, light color
  fills, white background inside the molecular inset, AP Biology high school
  level
- Palette continuity: glucose = warm gold, pyruvate = orange-red,
  acetaldehyde = salmon pink, ethanol = pale blue-green, ATP = bright
  yellow glow, NADH = violet glow, NAD+ = gray, CO2 = translucent white
  bubbles, yeast cell walls = tan/khaki, cytoplasm = pale cream

## Step 1 — Glucose Feedstock Preparation (Mashing and Wort Production)

**What is happening:** Malted barley is crushed and mixed with hot water in
a mash tun. Amylase enzymes from the malt break long starch chains into
fermentable sugars — primarily maltose (two glucose units) and free glucose.
The resulting sugar-rich liquid, called wort, is drained into a brew kettle,
boiled with hops, cooled, and transferred into the fermenter. This step
provides the raw fuel that yeast will consume.

**Key molecules to show:** Starch polymer chains (long spirals of linked
hexagons), amylase enzyme (wedge shape cleaving the chain), maltose
(two linked hexagonal rings), free glucose (single hexagonal ring glowing
warm gold), water molecules surrounding everything.

at molecular scale: long coiled starch polymer chains (light tan spirals of
linked hexagonal glucose units) being cleaved by wedge-shaped amylase enzymes
(blue-green) into short maltose disaccharides (two linked gold hexagons) and
free glucose monomers (single gold hexagons glowing warmly). Water molecules
(small transparent spheres) surround the reaction. Outside the inset, show
the golden wort flowing through a plate heat exchanger, steam rising, and

## Step 2 — Glycolysis Inside the Fermenter

**What is happening:** Yeast cells absorb glucose from the wort and
immediately begin glycolysis in their cytoplasm. This is a ten-enzyme
cascade that splits each 6-carbon glucose molecule into two 3-carbon
pyruvate molecules. The process nets 2 ATP molecules (the cell's energy
currency) and 2 NADH molecules (electron carriers that will be important
in Step 5). Glycolysis does not require oxygen — it proceeds whether or
not the fermenter is sealed.

**Key molecules to show:** Glucose (6-carbon gold hexagonal ring) entering
the yeast cell, a chain of enzyme complexes processing it through
intermediate forms, two pyruvate molecules (3-carbon, orange-red triangular
shapes) emerging at the end, 2 ATP (bright yellow glowing spheres), 2 NADH
(violet glowing spheres), faint mitochondria pushed to the cell periphery.

**Prompt:**

fermenter is cut away to reveal the liquid inside. Dozens of oval

visible tan cell walls and pale cream cytoplasm. Magnified inset circle in
the lower-right corner zooms into the cytoplasm of a single yeast cell at
molecular scale: a warm-gold hexagonal glucose molecule enters from the left
through a transporter channel in the cell membrane, then passes through a
conveyor-like chain of ten small enzyme complexes (shown as distinct colored
shapes arranged left to right), being progressively reshaped at each step.
At the midpoint, the 6-carbon molecule splits into two 3-carbon fragments.
At the far right of the chain, two orange-red triangular pyruvate molecules
emerge. Above the enzyme chain, two bright yellow glowing ATP spheres and
two violet glowing NADH spheres float upward, representing the energy

## Step 3 — Pyruvate Accumulation Under Anaerobic Conditions

**What is happening:** The brewer seals the fermenter, and the yeast
quickly consumes any remaining dissolved oxygen. With oxygen gone, the
mitochondrial electron transport chain shuts down — pyruvate cannot enter
the mitochondria for aerobic respiration. Pyruvate molecules accumulate in
the cytoplasm. Meanwhile, NADH also accumulates because it has no way to
donate its electrons to the ETC. This creates a metabolic bottleneck: if
NADH is not recycled back to NAD+, glycolysis will stall for lack of NAD+.
Fermentation is the cell's solution to this problem (Steps 4 and 5).

**Key molecules to show:** Multiple pyruvate molecules (orange-red
triangles) crowding the cytoplasm, NADH molecules (violet glow)
accumulating, mitochondria with closed/blocked pores (red X marks on the
outer membrane channels), NAD+ (gray, depleted) becoming scarce.

**Prompt:**

Please generate a new image. The same conical fermenter, now fully sealed with a tri-clamp lid and a

headspace blanket is visible above the wort surface as a faint haze. The
fermenter is cut away to show yeast cells suspended in darkening amber wort.
Magnified inset circle in the lower-right corner zooms into the cytoplasm of
a single yeast cell: the cytoplasm is now crowded with many orange-red
triangular pyruvate molecules that have nowhere to go. Several violet
glowing NADH spheres are accumulating in clusters. Along the right side of
the cell, two mitochondria are shown with their outer membrane channels
visibly blocked — the channel pores are closed with small red barrier marks,
and the interior cristae are dim and inactive. A few gray NAD+ spheres

A faint dotted arrow-like visual trail from pyruvate toward the blocked

## Step 4 — Decarboxylation: Acetaldehyde and CO2 Release

**What is happening:** The enzyme pyruvate decarboxylase (PDC) removes one
carbon from each 3-carbon pyruvate, releasing it as carbon dioxide (CO2).
What remains is acetaldehyde, a 2-carbon molecule. This is the step that
produces all the CO2 in beer — it carbonates the beer naturally, creates
the foam head, and is collected through blow-off tubes. Each original
glucose yields two pyruvates, so two CO2 molecules are released per glucose.
Acetaldehyde has a green-apple off-flavor that must be cleaned up in Step 5.

**Key molecules to show:** Pyruvate decarboxylase enzyme complex (large,
multi-lobed protein), pyruvate (orange-red triangle) entering the enzyme
active site, CO2 (small translucent white sphere) being released and
floating upward, acetaldehyde (2-carbon salmon-pink shape) exiting the
enzyme, the cofactor TPP (thiamine pyrophosphate, small green diamond)
nestled in the enzyme active site.

the wort surface, and the blow-off hose is actively bubbling into the

Magnified inset circle in the lower-right corner zooms into the cytoplasm
of a single yeast cell: at center stage, a large multi-lobed pyruvate
decarboxylase enzyme complex (rendered as a substantial blue-purple protein

## Step 5 — Ethanol Formation and NAD+ Regeneration (Conditioning)

**What is happening:** The enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) transfers
electrons from NADH to acetaldehyde, reducing it to ethanol — the alcohol
in beer. This reaction simultaneously regenerates NAD+ from NADH, which is
the true biological purpose of fermentation: recycling NAD+ so glycolysis
can continue producing ATP. Without this step, NADH would accumulate,
NAD+ would run out, and glycolysis would stop. The yeast eventually
flocculates (clumps and settles) to the bottom cone. The bright beer is
transferred to a conditioning tank where residual acetaldehyde is cleaned
up, CO2 is captured, and flavors mature.

**Key molecules to show:** Alcohol dehydrogenase enzyme (large, multi-lobed
protein), acetaldehyde (salmon-pink V-shape) entering the active site,
NADH (violet glow) donating electrons (shown as a light trail transferring
from NADH to acetaldehyde), ethanol (pale blue-green small molecule)
exiting, NAD+ (now restored to gray, ready for reuse) departing the enzyme,
a circular recycling motif showing NAD+ flowing back toward the glycolysis
enzymes from Step 2.

**Prompt:**

side of the fermenter to a horizontal conditioning tank nearby. A CO2
capture dome sits on top collecting residual gas. The fermenter is cut away
